Amsterdam · Getting Around
Transport that actually works
From Schiphol: take the NS train to Amsterdam Centraal — 17 min, ~€5. Skip taxis, they're expensive.
Credit card tap works on GVB trams and metro — tap in AND tap out or you get charged the max fare.
For Zaanse Schans: buy a one-way NS ticket (~€4 each way), not a day pass — it's cheaper for a single trip out of the city.
Bikes have absolute right of way — always look both ways before stepping off a curb. Tram tracks are also a hazard for ankles.
The GVB app shows real-time tram/metro arrivals and lets you plan routes easily.
🧀
Amsterdam · Food & Culture
Eat like a local, not a tourist
Albert Cuyp Market is the real deal — cheese, stroopwafels, fresh herring, Dutch pancakes. Way cheaper than tourist spots near Dam Square.
"Coffee shops" sell cannabis (18+, ID required). "Cafés" or "eetcafé" are for actual coffee and food — know the difference.
Jordaan neighbourhood cafés are 25–35% cheaper than anything near Dam Square — and far more atmospheric.
Try a broodje haring (raw herring sandwich) from a street stall — it's a quintessential Amsterdam experience and costs ~€3.
Most places accept card. Keep €20–30 cash for markets and smaller canal-side cafés.
Floating Flower Market (Bloemenmarkt) is tourist-facing but worth a quick stroll — it's on a canal, 5 min from Rijksmuseum.
🎨
Amsterdam · Museums & Sights
Get the most out of your bookings
Rijksmuseum: aim for the Night Watch first (main hall, 2nd floor) — crowds build up as the day goes on. 2–3 hrs is ideal.
Van Gogh Museum: 15–30 min early arrival is fine. The self-portrait room gets packed — go there first. Audio guide is worth it.
Anne Frank House tickets often sell out weeks ahead — but you have yours. Arrive 10 min early and have your confirmation ready.
The I Amsterdam sign near Rijksmuseum is popular for photos — best angle is from across the reflecting pool, early morning or late afternoon for light.
Canals are most beautiful in the golden hour (7–9 PM in late May) — great evening walk in Jordaan after the museums.

Belgium · Key Things to Know
Brussels, Ghent & Bruges
Belgium has 3 official languages — French in Brussels, Dutch (Flemish) in Ghent/Bruges. Train stations show both names: Bruges = Brugge, Ghent = Gent. Check both spellings on boards.
Brussels is very walkable from your hotel — Grand Place, Manneken Pis, and Rue Neuve shopping are all under 10 min on foot.
Brussels Midi (your station) is the main international hub. Brussels Central and Brussels North are different stations — don't confuse them.
Day trip trains: no advance booking needed for Brussels → Ghent → Bruges — just buy at the station or SNCB app on the day.
Most shops and some restaurants close on Sundays, even in tourist areas. Plan meals accordingly.
Around Gare du Midi, move through quickly and keep bags close — it can be rough around the station perimeter at night.

Belgium · Food & Drink
The real Belgian experience
Belgian beer: each beer has its own specific glass — this is taken seriously. Never take the glass home from a bar (seriously, it's not done).
Don't eat waffles or moules-frites right on Grand Place — walk 2 blocks away and prices drop significantly, quality goes up.
Pierre Marcolini is the gold standard for chocolate in Brussels — he selects his own cocoa beans. Near Grand Place.
Delirium Café has 2,000+ beers and holds a Guinness World Record — fun atmosphere, just watch your tab.
In Bruges: De Halve Maan is the only working brewery in the city centre — the tour is worth it, ends with a free beer.
Chocolate Line in Bruges is creative and unusual — try the wasabi or bacon chocolate if you're feeling adventurous.

Paris · Metro in 2026
Big changes since 2024 — read this
Paper tickets are completely gone as of 2025. You need a Navigo Easy card (€2 for the card, load tickets onto it) or use the Bonjour RATP app on your phone.
Metro directions are named by the last station on the line, not your destination. Download Citymapper — it tells you exactly which direction to go.
From Gare du Nord to Hotel Touring: take Metro Line 4 towards Montrouge, 2 stops to Cadet or Poissonnière. About 5 min.
For CDG airport return: take RER B from Gare du Nord (~€11.80, 35 min). Do not take a taxi — flat rate is €56 and Uber often surges to €80+.
Validate your ticket every time you enter — inspectors appear without warning and fines are on the spot.
Screenshot your Citymapper directions before going underground — many stations have spotty signal.

Paris · Sights & Timing
Beat the crowds at each spot
Trocadéro is the best Eiffel Tower photo spot — cross the Seine and shoot from the esplanade. Evening light (7–9 PM in May) is golden. At night the tower sparkles on the hour.
Sacré-Cœur: arrive before 9 AM to have the steps mostly to yourself and catch the morning light over Paris. It's magical.
Louvre at 5 PM is genuinely smart — crowds thin out significantly. The museum stays open until 9:45 PM on Fridays. Mona Lisa is in Salle 711 (Room 711), Denon Wing.
Notre-Dame exterior is stunning even during restoration — the Seine walk around Île de la Cité is one of the best in Paris.
Galeries Lafayette rooftop terrace is completely free — great 360° city view including Eiffel Tower. Go up via the 7th floor lift.
Rue de l'Abreuvoir in Montmartre: best in early morning before 9 AM — no crowds, beautiful light, very photogenic.

Paris · Food Rules
Eat well, spend less
Always say "Bonjour Madame/Monsieur" when entering any shop, café, or restaurant. Not doing this is considered rude — it genuinely changes how you're treated.
Walk 2–3 blocks from any major tourist sight and prices drop 40–60%. The best food is almost never on the tourist strip.
Lunch is the main meal in France — many good restaurants do a "plat du jour" (dish of the day) for €12–15 that costs €25+ at dinner.
Tipping: not expected the way it is in Canada. Round up or leave €2–3 for good service — that's generous by local standards.
Bakeries open from 7 AM — grab a croissant (plain, not almond) and café au lait for under €4. Best cheap breakfast in the world.
No breakfast on Sundays at Hotel Touring (May 31 checkout day) — head to any nearby boulangerie for a final French breakfast.

Safety Across All 3 Countries
What actually matters
Pickpocketing is the main risk everywhere — not violent crime. Use a cross-body bag or keep wallet in a front pocket. Never in a back pocket.
Paris scams to know: friendship bracelet (someone ties it on your wrist, demands payment — say "Non" firmly and walk away), petition signers, three-cup game. If a stranger approaches you, 99% chance it's a scam.
On trains and metro: sit away from the doors. Phone theft at doors ("grab and go") is the most common theft on public transport.
Keep a photo of your passport, both sides of all cards, and hotel addresses saved offline on your phone.
Emergency number across all EU countries: 112. Works everywhere, even without a SIM card.
Canadian Embassy Brussels: +32 2 741 0611. Canadian Embassy Paris: +33 1 44 43 29 00.

Money & Cards
For this specific trip
KOHO card is great for travel — no foreign transaction fees. Use it as your main spending card.
Keep €50–80 cash at all times — markets, canal boat tours, some Bruges attractions, and small cafés are often cash-only or have card minimums.
Best Western Amsterdam: payment is due at check-in. Put the hold on Visa as planned — don't let the card expire or hit its limit beforehand.
Amex acceptance in Europe is only 70–80%. Always have a Visa or Mastercard as backup.
Use bank ATMs in busy, well-lit locations only. Avoid standalone ATMs at tourist attractions — higher fees and occasional skimmers.
Notify your bank before departure (especially for cards not yet flagged for Europe travel).
